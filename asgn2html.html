Life Expectancy & Population - Japan

Life Expectancy is the statistical measure of the average time an organism is expected to live based on the year of its birth. It has been varyinig since the beginning of life. Japan is well-known for having the highest life expectancy among the G7 countries, yet in the early 1960s, Japanese life expectancy was the lowest of any G7 country. It is believed that the change in diet and several other factors improved their life expectancy. Thus, we can see differences in average life expectancy in different prefectures of Japan, due to the differences in lifestyle.

Life Expectancy by Prefectures

As observed, Akita and Aomori have the lowest life expectancy, and they are both in the Tohoku(Northeastern) Region. On the other hand, Shiga, Nagano and Fukui have the highest life expectancy, and 2 of them are located in the Chubu(Central) Region, with Shiga a little towards the west. These data convey something about the difference in how people live in different region, and how it affects their life expectancy.


We can look further into these demographics by analysing life expectancy between both genders.

Life Expectancy of each prefecture, by Gender

The average life expectancy for Japanese women is 87.01, whereas for male it is 80.77.

Japanese women have been the world's longest-lived females for the 10th year running, however it is not limited to Japan where women live longer than men. Everywhere in the world, women live longer than men, however the evidence for the reason is limited. Part of the evidence shows that differences in chromosomes and hormones between men and women affect longevity. For instance, males tend to have more fat surrounuding the organs, whereas women tend to have more fat sitting directly under the skin. This is due to the estrogen produced by females, and this affects longevity because visceral fats predicts cardiovascular diseases.

Generally, population density does affect life expectancy, because socioeconomic background of citizens depends on GDP per capita.

Let's observe if there are any connections between each prefecture's population density and their life expectancy.

Population by Prefecture

Looking at Shiga, Nagano, Fukui, Akita and Aomori, all 5 have similar population density (80-150). We can conclude that population density does not affect each prefecture's lifespan.

In retrospect, what makes their longevity greater or lesser then?

Let's look into the factors that affect life expectancy.

Factors affecting Life Expectancy

In Japan, all other factors are equally on standard - the only varying factor is the lifestyle.

Lifestyle includes diet, physical activity, mental health, etc..



Aomori, as well as having the lowest life expectancy, is also above average for deaths by lifestyle-habit connected ailments like cancer, stroke and heart attack. This is due to their extremely high salt consumption.
Nagano, now the prefecture with the longest life expectancy in Japan, once led the nation in strokes due to excessive consumption of salt. However, an aggressive government campaign was mounted to reduce salt intake; within a decade, the effects of the campaign were felt, and the results are clearly apparent.
